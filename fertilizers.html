Fertilizers - for acriculture, gardeners & wholesalers

Directly from producers from many countries, we import various types of fertilizers that meet the highest standards and are approved for use in agriculture in the European Union.

Potassium fertilisers - belong to the group of mineral fertilisers where the leading ingredient is K+ potassium.

These fertilisers improve the water balance of plants and help them adapt to drought. By absorbing potassium into the tissues, plants become stronger and less susceptible to pests and fungal pathogens. This makes the plants absorb carbon dioxide better and use light more efficiently, and they are also more resistant to frost.

Potassium fertilisers are divided into :

  • Chloride - used for plants that are not sensitive to excess chlorine, where the potassium is in the form of potassium chloride (KCl)
  • Sulphate - potassium is present in the form of potassium sulphate. These can be used for all plants.
Phosphorus fertilisers are mineral fertilisers whose main nutrient is phosphorus.

It is a macronutrient in plant nutrition. These fertilisers increase the yield to a small extent, but significantly improve the quality of the crop. They are responsible for the colour and size of the flowers. When phosphorus fertiliser is applied, the fruit is more shapely and has better colour. The fertiliser also influences the rooting process of the plant and additionally influences the efficiency of nitrogen fertilisation.

Phosphorus fertilisers are divided according to their speed of action:

  • Fast-acting
  • Water soluble
  • Slow-acting / Very slow-acting
  • Soluble in weak / strong acids

Why phosphorus?

Phosphorus plays a very important role in plant development. It is a building block of leaves, flowers and fruit. It is not available in natural form, so only artificial fertiliser is used. On fertile and fertile soils with good structure, not much phosphorus is needed, but on heavy, clayey and wet soils, fertilisation is very important. Phosphorus compounds found in such soils are not available to plants.

Nitrogen fertilisers - belong to the group based on readily available nitrogen compounds.

Here we distinguish 3 sub-groups: fast-acting - nitrate - slow-acting - ammonium long-acting - amide, e.g. urea N46 in our offer Nitrogen is of the greatest importance for the development of plants. It is responsible for increasing green mass and seed yield. In the case of a nitrogen deficiency, plants have poor tillering and weak and rapidly falling leaves. In addition, the quality of the crop decreases, there is a shortening and weakening of stems and the growing season is shortened.

With regard to the form of nitrogen, nitrogen fertilisers are divided into:

  • Ammonium fertilisers - based on ammonium sulphate. They have a strong acidifying effect, used on alkaline or neutral soils. They dissolve very well in water and are therefore applied foliarly.
  • Nitrate fertilisers - used for late top dressing. Nitrate comes in different types and the criterion for division is the composition, as it can be combined with different minerals.
  • Ammonium nitrate fertilisers - easy to apply and quickly soluble in water. They are used to strengthen the soil before sowing or planting. Saltpeter has a slightly acidic reaction and can therefore affect the pH level of the soil.
  • Amide fertilisers
Multi-nutrient fertilisers are mineral fertilisers that contain two or more of the primary plant nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium).

The ratio of N:P:K in the applied fertiliser has to be adapted to the needs by establishing the right proportions between the individual components. A distinction is made between mixed compound fertilisers, i.e. those obtained by mechanically mixing the ingredients (e.g. superphosphate with ammonium sulphate) and those obtained by chemical reactions (e.g. ammonium phosphates).
Typically, ammonium phosphates are mixed with potassium salts. This results in a two-component fertiliser (NP) and a three-component fertiliser (NPK). The use of compound fertilisers reduces labour intensity as it reduces the number of fertilisation operations.
